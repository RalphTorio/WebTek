Transculturation

A process of cultural transformation known as transculturation is characterized by the entrance of new cultural components and the loss or modification of preexisting ones. Essentially, it is a type of socialization and, thus, is a continual process of cultural learning and relearning. Transculturation comprises the interactions between cultures through which a person continuously broadens their knowledge of and aptitude for various cultures. It is challenging to identify and credit the source when using this form because it takes components from several civilizations and combines them.

Examples:

-The arrival of migratory movements from one location to another frequently modifies cultural patterns from one place. Many nations, particularly Latin America, attribute their current traits to the tribes that settled there. In this way, it's possible that a population, an even more significant one that currently resides in a nation with specific rules, would arrive, and some of the rules of a foreign cultural group would be absorbed. For instance, almost every city has a Chinatown

-Through successful economic and cultural interactions, a new cultural form is introduced. It frequently happens because the individuals who adopt the new forms believe the new patterns are superior, but it can also occur solely due to market forces. For instance, the spread of new technology changed the music that is listened to in most western countries, allowing for the simultaneous listening of dozens of musicians in various locations.
